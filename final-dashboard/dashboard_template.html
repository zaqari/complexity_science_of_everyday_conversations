Monolingual English Conversations

Summary and report
Summary and report

Concepts shared between utterances have a durative, though temporally limited effect. What concepts are expressed by any one speaker appear to more strongly influence/are easier to recover from the same speaker's subsequent utterances, though utterances clearly influence the concepts expressed by both the "self" and "others" during interaction. The change in Entropy (see the top figure below, and the upper figure for each individual corpus) is exponential. Notably, as time progresses the difference in how much an utterance $x$ influences a speaker's own contributions to conversation and their partners' shrinks exponentially as well (see the lower figure below).

Exponential growth parameters for $\alpha$ are as follows

  • self: ($\alpha=-.199, SE=.001, t(519389)=-144.27, p < 1e^{-9}$)
  • other: ($\alpha=-.141, SE=.001, t(509862)=-79.32, p< 1e^{-9}$)
  • other-self: ($\alpha=-.178, SE=.002, t(496276)=-114.87, p< 1e^{-9}$)

CANDOR Exploratory Study

Summary and report

Results indicate that it is not merely semantic similarity that facilitates terminal perceptions of shared perspective and positive assessment of one's discursive partners, but the degree to which speakers and partners' responses follow population norms for how much information is carried over from one utterance to the next.

People do not look for similarity, they look for synergy--the ability to jointly move from one set of concepts to the next with their partner as a marker of conversational quality.

Multilingual Conversations Across Languages

Summary and report

Analyses of 8 additional languages, plus a subset of the English conversation data, replicates the same temporal and interpersonal characteristics of the English monolingual study.

Exponential growth parameters for $\alpha$ are as follows
